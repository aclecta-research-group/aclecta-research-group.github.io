Delving Deeper into the Bowels of Strategy Games

   In our previous article, available here, a comparison was drawn between Red Team/Blue Team exercises and Magic: the Gathering in the hopes of increasing the exercise's effectiveness. Probably the most important element discussed was the notion of the Metagame, the game outside the Game or the state of strategy that exists outside of any single match that dictates the card choices a highly skilled Magic player will make to increase his or her effectiveness against a random-but equally-skilled-opponent. The parallel that holds here is that an outside Red Team can conceivably predict weakness in the Blue Team's infrastructure based upon what is considered "out of bounds" for the sake of the exercise. If you haven't checked out the article and you're unfamiliar with the game, take a moment to read it before continuing. What follows below skips over the previous explanations and introduces a few more ideas.

   Combat in Magic, consists of "creatures" that are summoned from anywhere in the Multiverse to attack your opponent. Dragons make ferocious attackers, but take a while to deploy. Artifacts (magical machinery) can be deployed for a variety of reasons, from ramping up resource production (in order to get your Dragons out faster) to destroying your opponents creatures (to punish your opponent for spending so many resources on one dragon). Casting Fireballs at your opponents face will sometimes win games, but the same fireball directed at Eldritch Abomination might save you from having your face eaten off. All of these elements (and many more besides) work together to create a rich strategic environment, where analyzing your opponent's position is as crucial to victory as proper sequencing the deployment of your own assets.

   In Magic, it isn't wise to ignore your opponent and what he or she is trying to do to win. A "threat" is any card or combination of cards that if left unchecked will win the game. The "answer" to a threat is "removal", which is a card that takes the threat out of play. That dragon that was so fierce? Casting it straight into the pits of Hades renders it useless. That artifact that deals you 2 points of damage once per turn? If you disenchant it, it goes away.

   In the paper/rock/scissors of the Metagame, the most simplified breakdown consists of 3 archetypes: aggro, control, & combo. Aggro, short for aggressive, is a deck that tries to eliminate the opponent before they can do anything meaningful–ending the game as quickly as possible in a full out assault. This type of deck packs few answers and instead focuses on presenting more threats, more quickly than any opponent can hope to answer them. Control is exactly what it sounds like. This type of deck seeks to utilize various types of resource advantage while denying the opponent the opportunity utilize resources committed to the board. Combo is short for "combination" and attempts to present some 2 or 3 card interaction that will use an in-game loop to generate infinite health, infinite damage, infinite spells, or some mix thereof. Historically, this type of deck is a glass cannon and loses if the opponent can prevent the cards from interacting as they should.

   As new players learn the various elements of strategy and begin to win more, it's a pretty typical experience for the player to see a significant increase in their win rate then shortly thereafter have a precipitous drop in win rate. The increase is due to a more thorough understanding of things like board state, resource conversion ratios, deck construction techniques, etc. The drop that occurs afterwards is because they don't yet see the next important lesson:

There are wrong answers, but there are no wrong threats.

   If the opponent is presenting one major threat per turn, then the first time a threat goes unanswered the game is likely decided at that time. It may take several more turns for the win to be consolidated, but that fact is the battle that wins or loses the war. Addressing the previous paragraph, intermediate level players usually decide to make their decks more resourceful, less linear, and more capable of dealing with complex board states. All of these goals are worthy of pursuit. However, these intermediate players ignore what helped them to increase their win rate: unanswered threats win games, but answering threats only staves off a loss. That distinction is a very fine point that is easy to miss.

That distinction is what separates the local weekend grinders from players on the verge of making the Pros.

That distinction is why, in the typical Red Team/Blue Team exercise, the Red Team usually comes out on top.

A Moat, an Archangel with a flaming sword, and a Black Hat Hacker walk into a bar...

   As they sit down, the Moat picks up his argument from earlier, saying to the Angel, "Sure, you can fly over me but I'm backed up by the solid walls of my castle, and the King's archer's can pick you off before you get close."
   The Angel ponders this for a moment and says, "You're absolutely correct. But if I hit you 10 minutes before shift change, when the archers are all tired of standing watch and only thinking of going home to soft beds and beautiful wives, your deterrence rate will drop significantly. It is at that point, that the Hosts will descend. No static defense is impenetrable."
At this point, the Hacker looks up from his phone and starts laughing. The Moat and the Archangel both look at him like he's lost his mind. Eventually the Angel draws is sword and says, "Listen, fool. God may suffer foolishness, but I do not. What is the jest that drives your laughter?"
The Hacker stands up, drops a $20 on the bar, and says "This rounds on me. While you were busy theorycrafting tactics and countermeasures, I hacked both your email accounts, changed your passwords, and sent various orders in your names. The King's archers are on high alert for an incoming Angel assault, posted to every 2nd story window, parapet, and wall station in the city, while the dark elves are creeping up from below. Likewise, the Angels are amassing to attack for the mock exercise, but they're in the wrong city, on the wrong coast of the Kingdom.
   "The moral here, boys? Don't let the game become the point of the game. If you hurry, you might make it to the capital before the invasion is over."
   And with that, he disappeared into the darkness.

   To say that there are no wrong threats implies that all attacks are equally profitable. This implication is untrue, both in Red Team exercises and in real world black hat operations. Furthermore, the implication is unintended. What is intended is that perfect operational security is impossible. Computers suffer from the failures of their designers. Staff is limited by funding, availability, or even just experience with the problems at hand. Perhaps what needs to be explored is how the Blue Team can cease waiting behind a wall and a moat, and instead take proactive countermeasures–or even make reactive counterassaults that prey upon the weaknesses of the Red Team's strategy.

   How that might be implemented remains to be seen.
