Red Team Exercises: Improving InfoSec through Gaming

   Billion dollar industrial firms face a host of security threats. From angry employees wanting to exact revenge for perceived insults to Black Hat criminals attacking a firms web presence and digital infrastructure, the varieties of potential malicious actors span a wide spectrum. Beyond Penetration Testing (testing one's information security safeguards), Red Team/Blue Team exercises have become something of the norm for high value enterprises. Borrowed from the military, a Red Team/Blue Team exercise involves one team acting as an attacker (the Red Team, usually an external group hired for that specific purpose) while the Blue Team defends the company's assets. This set up allows for a more thorough understanding of the Blue team's inherent strengths and weaknesses than is available in a simple PenTest. The potential exists that the Red Team fails, not because of the work of the Blue Team, but because it failed to find the right network exploit within the time frame of the exercise. Although this suggests that the Blue Team was properly prepared for the attack, it does not prove such. Getting "lucky" in that exercise could prove very detrimental should a determined malicious hacker spend many more hours to find the access that the Red Team could not. An examination of other non-Zero Sum Games could prove profitable in expanding the effectiveness of a Red Team exercise by ensuring that a multi-axial approach to strategy is implemented in the assault.

   One of the most successful games in the past 25 years is a collectible card game called Magic: the Gathering (or "Magic" for short). In Magic, each player assumes the role of a nearly omnipotent wizard, known as a Planeswalker, who battles for mastery and control of his or her own section of the Multiverse. Consisting of 60 card decks designed by the players themselves, the game combines traditional high fantasy for stylistic and design elements with tactics and strategy of Chess, Poker, & Go for game play elements, where each player must balance competing requirements in an attempt to amass a board position wherein his opponent is exhausted of any recourse. Although there is a score system (consisting of life totals), games are won or lost based upon the tactical and strategic decisions made both during game play and even before the game began, during deck construction. Choosing to utilize a quick strike force that decapitates the opponent before he or she can enact his or her strategy will sometimes work, but against an opponent prepared for the tactic, such a plan crumbles before it can even begin. In Red Team exercises, social engineering a target might prevent hours of wasted effort in network penetration. Or, it may tip off the target that something questionable is ongoing. In Magic, denying the opponent access to crucial resources is a nasty strategy that has caused much salt, rage, and table-flipping over the years, but it is still used because it works. Physically infiltrating a network access point requires stealth, deceit, or an effective diversion, but it is worth the effort for the Red Team if all inbound connections are prevented by firewall rule.

   Magic has a Pro Tour, including a Word Championship that pays out $250,000 to its winner. Such prizes incite levels of competition that go far beyond just playing the best cards in the hopes of winning. Top Magic professionals spend as many hours per year prepping for tournament play as do professional athletes in the NFL, NBA, or MLB. This amount of study and preparation leads to an interesting meta-game of Paper/Rock/Scissors. The best deck from last month can be exploited by a certain strategy. Does the professional play that deck? Or the deck that beats that deck? Or even, the deck that beats that deck? In Red Teaming, a set of goals are established prior to the exercise, typically of the "capture the flag" variety. Likewise, certain methods, networks, departments, or physical locations are "out of bounds" and not targetable during the exercise. Within those constraints, anything is allowed. The Blue team sets the stage by pre-deploying network and physical safeguards. The Red Team is likely to reconnitor all of these during its initial assessment of the exercise. Even if the Blue Team doesn't tip its hand unintentionally, a skilled Red Team can deduce weaknesses by what's allowed or disallowed. This type of meta-gaming may be frowned upon by the Blue Team that has a vested interest in winning a fair fight, but it should not be a fair fight.

   A malicious actor doesn't care about fair-he is only concerned with whatever it is that drives his malice. The whole purpose of a Red Team/Blue Team exercise is to investigate preparation for, and responsiveness to, Black Hats. Coming to terms with the meta-game of the InfoSec world is as crucial an exercise as anything else that the Red Team will attempt. Modelling this fact and exploring this model is an ongoing work, both here at Aclecta and at InfoSec firms worldwide.
